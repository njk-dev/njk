*on monks, mountains, and milk cartons, in the week of the feast of Saint Charbel Makhlouf.*
{.preface}

I took Charbel for my confirmation name. Part of the reason is a saint. The rest is a grandmother.

This past December, Pope Leo XIV stood in a low stone vault in the mountains of Lebanon and asked: what is the legacy of a man who wrote nothing, who lived a hidden and silent life, yet whose fame spread throughout the world?[^1]

He was standing at the tomb of Saint Charbel Makhlouf. No pope had ever stood there before.

The man was baptized Youssef in 1828. He took the name of an ancient martyr, Charbel, when he entered the monastery.[^martyr]

Drawn to greater solitude, Charbel asked permission to leave for the hermitage. The superior told him to wait, and prayed for a sign. One night in 1875, while Charbel was still at the monastery of Saint Maroun, his lamp ran dry. He asked a young brother to refill it. The brother poured in water for a joke, then hid behind the door to watch. Charbel thanked him, lit the lamp, and went on working by its flame. The superior came, dipped a finger into the burning lamp, and tasted it: water.[^lamp] That year Charbel went up the mountain.

For twenty-three years as a hermit, Charbel slept a few hours a night and ate one meal a day. His life centered on the Mass he celebrated at noon. The rest of the day he spent in silent prayer, either preparing himself to offer the Eucharist or giving thanks for having offered it.

## the light

Charbel gave his soul to God on Christmas Eve of 1898. Immediately after he was laid to rest, a brilliant light rose from the place of his tomb. It appeared first to the poor and the overlooked. It appeared to women who could not read. It appeared finally to the Muslim administrator of the region and his soldiers. The light went before them until it disappeared at the monastery door. Night after night, Christians and Muslims saw the light and made pilgrimage to the tomb. His brother monks were the most doubting; only after they exhumed the body of Father Charbel and found it incorrupt did they begin to believe.[^3]

> God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the wise, God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong, God chose what is low and despised in the world, even things that are not, to bring to nothing things that are. <cite>— 1 Corinthians 1:27–28</cite>

Few ever saw Saint Charbel's face. He lived in solitude and kept his eyes toward the ground, lifting them only before Christ in the Blessed Sacrament.

> In the tender compassion of our God\
> the dawn from on high shall break upon us, <cite>— Luke 1:78</cite>

Rome took longer. Charbel was beatified by Pope Saint Paul VI in the closing days of the Second Vatican Council. While the Church debated its future, Paul VI held up a silent hermit: the Church, he told the Council, also needs men of prayer and hidden virtue, whose poverty, penance, and asceticism liberate the soul in its ascent to God. Twelve years later the same pope canonized him, admitting that modern man can scarcely comprehend so severe a life.[^2]

The Maronite Church long kept his feast on July 23, the anniversary not of his death but of his priestly ordination, the day he handed his life over. Now the feast falls on the third Sunday of July; the Latin Church keeps July 24.[^5] No official calendar keeps the 22nd. To a woman healed at his intercession in 1993, the saint himself is said to have spoken: come to the hermitage on the 22nd, and pray.[^4] This Wednesday, July 22, while we fast, thousands climb the mountain road to Annaya, as they do on the 22nd of every month.

## the quiet

Now, my grandmother. Helen Rose Latuff was the daughter of Lebanese Maronite immigrants. She married an Irish Catholic in the Latin Church: one household breathing with both lungs, East and West.[^6] She never spoke of the saint, and rarely of Lebanon.

In the summers I would join her and the other women who made the food and hosted the funeral receptions. I watched: the flowers placed in the church, the tidying between the big twice-yearly cleanings. The prayer circles. The dinners put together and dropped off when someone was ill.

Once a month it was the Dorothy Day Center in St. Paul. I helped the way a kid helps: underfoot while the women cooked. When it came time to serve, I sat on a stool and handed out the cartons of milk as the room came through, cafeteria style. I didn't quite understand the men and women who were there. Wouldn't they rather eat at home?

From the stool I watched my grandmother smile the whole length of the line, with her sister, my great-aunt Jane, beside her. My grandmother had a greeting and a joke for everyone. She knew many of the people by name, and they knew her. And when the last tray was served, she came around the counter into the dining room, for the conversations and the hugs. She could have just made the food and served it. No one would have complained.

## the legacy

Pope Leo answered his own question: the Holy Spirit formed Charbel so that he could teach those who live without God how to pray, those who live immersed in noise how to be silent, those who live ostentatiously how to be modest, and those who seek riches how to be poor. Before Pope Leo left the mountain, he offered a lamp — a symbol, he said, of the light God kindled there through Charbel. And a charge: keep walking in the light of the Lord.[^1]

Few of us are called to the mountain.

Charbel's cell was dark for twenty-three years, and now everyone sees it.

Not many outside the parish ever knew Helen. She came around the counter anyway.

The Father saw Charbel. The Father saw Helen. The Father sees us.[^design]

> to shine on those who dwell in darkness and the shadow of death,\
> and to guide our feet into the way of peace. <cite>— Luke 1:79</cite>

I took the name for both of them.
