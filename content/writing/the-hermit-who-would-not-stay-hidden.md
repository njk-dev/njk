*A longer companion to a short reflection I shared with my Wednesday fasting group, for the feast of St. Charbel Makhlouf.*
{.preface}

This past December, Pope Leo XIV stood at a tomb in the mountains of Lebanon and asked a question: what is the legacy of a man who wrote nothing, who lived a hidden and silent life — and whose fame somehow spread throughout the world?

He was standing at the grave of St. Charbel Makhlouf, and he was the first pope ever to do so. Which is itself a strange fact. Popes had been talking about this obscure hermit for sixty years. Paul VI interrupted the closing days of the Second Vatican Council to beatify him, telling a Church busy debating its future that it also needed men who give themselves to unceasing prayer and freely accepted penance.

Strong words for a man whose entire résumé fits in one sentence: he was a monk, and then he was a hermit, and then he died.

## the sent man

Youssef Makhlouf was born in 1828 in Bekaa Kafra, the highest village in Lebanon. At twenty-three he walked away from home, entered the Lebanese Maronite Order, and never returned. Sixteen years in community at the Monastery of St. Maron in Annaya, then the thing he'd wanted all along: a hermitage up the hill, and twenty-three years in it. One meal a day. Five hours of sleep on a mat, a block of wood for a pillow. Silence, fields, and the Mass.

What strikes me most is not the austerity. It's that he did all of it under obedience. Charbel asked permission for everything, including visits from his own family. When he died, his superior wrote in the monastery's death register that his obedience seemed "angelic and not human."[^1] He didn't storm off to a mountain to do Catholicism his own way. He was sent there, and he stayed sent.

## the light

He suffered a stroke in December 1898 while celebrating the Liturgy — mid-prayer, at the words "Father of Truth" — and died on Christmas Eve. They buried him in the snow. And then the villagers began to see a light over his grave, night after night. Christians, and their Muslim neighbors too. To this day the monastery at Annaya draws millions to the tomb of a man who spent seventy years trying to disappear.

> Lebanon is more than a country — it is a message. <cite>— St. John Paul II[^2]</cite>

I have a personal stake in this. Charbel is my confirmation saint, and I chose him partly for my grandmother — a Maronite girl put on a boat across the ocean as a young teen, sent ahead to wait for the rest of the family to follow. She carried Lebanon's quiet faith with her, in a place not quite her own, without fanfare, for decades. I doubt she thought anyone was watching. Two generations later, I'm still living off the light.

Our little group fasts on Wednesdays. Nobody sees it. That's the design. But Charbel's cell was dark for twenty-three years, and God made it a beacon. Whatever you're offering quietly — keep offering it. Somebody downstream will live off the light.
